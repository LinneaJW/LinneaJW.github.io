Aurora borealis was named after both Aurora, Roman goddess of the dawn, and Boreas, Greek god of the north wind. In Finland, the aurora borealis is called revontulet - fox fires. Finnish folklore tells of a magical fox sweeping his tail across the snow and sending sparks up into the sky creating the lights.

Aurora borealis AKA the northern lights are visible in upper latitudes nearest the North Pole. Southern lights AKA aurora australis occur in lower latitudes closer to the South Pole at the same time as the northern lights occur.

Auroras appear in different shapes. They can be a mild glow near the horizon, with undiminished stars visible through the glow. Auroras may be patches or surfaces that look like clouds. Auroras may be arcs curving across the sky. Rays are light and dark color stripes across arcs. Coronas diverge from a single point to cover much of the sky. The most common type of aurora resembles a glowing curtain. A new kind of aurora, named dune, appears like frozen horizontal waves glowing across the sky.

Charged particles from the Sun trapped in Earth’s magnetic field pass through Earth’s upper atmosphere. These particles knock electrons off atoms in the atmosphere, ionizing them to trigger beautiful glowing auroras. Solar particles at the peak of the 11-year sunspot cycle create the most frequent auroras. The next solar maximum, when the Sun is at peak activity, is predicted to occur in July 2025.

Altitude and the kind of atoms involved determine aurora colors. If ions strike oxygen atoms high in the atmosphere, the interaction produces a red glow. The most familiar green color occurs as ions strike oxygen at lower altitudes. Reddish and bluish light often in the lower fringes of auroras is produced by ions striking atoms of nitrogen. Ions striking hydrogen and helium atoms can produce blue and purple auroras.

Aurora noise sounds like a hissing or crackling. This noise begins about 230 feet above the Earth's surface. Charged particles in an inversion layer of the atmosphere formed on cold nights cause the noise. When particles from the Sun hit the inversion layer, charged particles discharge making the noise.

Scientists have observed auroras on other planets in our solar system and outside of our solar system. Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, Venus, and Mars have auroras. An extra-solar aurora was discovered in July 2015 over a brown dwarf star. This mainly red aurora was a million times brighter than the northern lights.

To enjoy the dancing northern lights in person requires planning and good luck with getting the weather to cooperate. Aurora forecasts are available. Clear night sky during active sunspot periods is ideal. But every year near equinoxes in March and September, the Earth’s magnetic field lets more solar particles interact with the atmosphere, creating aurora seasons! Iceland is best from late August to early April. Iceland has buubble lodges which are transparent spaces to star gaze and see auroras. Locations in various countries have spectacular ice hotels, offering aurora viewing opportunities themselves or nearby. In the United States Fairbanks Alaska is the best place for aurora viewing. Fairbanks and Yellowknife Canada season is late August to early April. Northern Sweden, Tromsø and the Lofoten islands in Norway, and Finland are all best mid-September to late March. Greenland is best mid-August to late April.


See the aurora light show from the comfort of home! Real-time videos best capture the reality factor of being there. Time-lapse videos show fast dancing lights. See the aurora australis from the International Space Station (ISS) as though you are aboard.
