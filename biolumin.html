Biolulminescence, an often neon blue glow, is generated by living organisms. Many beaches in southern California had biolumninescence recently, Playa Del Rey, Manhattan Beach, San Clemente, and Hermosa Beach to name a few. In and near San Diego California this effect usually takes place about every other year. It is created when phytoplankton called dinoflagellates crash in the tide. They react when jostled causing bioluminescence. During the day their pigmentation can make the water deep red, brown or orange - the red tide.

Bioluminesence in Jervis Bay in New South Wales Australia also comes from bioluminescent plankton. They emit a strong blue tinted glow due to a chemical reaction. When red algae are visible in the day, likelihood of bioluminescence in the evening is greater. In Jervis Bay nutrients flow in following rain, which triggers a phytoplankton bloom. A few calm and sunny days after a good rain the bioluminescence should appear at night. Bioluminescence is best observed in total darkness later at night.

Sea sparkle only came to Tasmania in 1994. The neon blooms of Noctiluca scintillans, a type of sea algae, create the bioluminescence here. Their coming is likely due to warmer water from climate change and more polluted water - agricultural and industrial wastewater - with what serve as nutrients to them. They pose a risk to local marine life since blooms of this scale can take all the available nutrients and starve other organisms.

Toyoma Bay in Japan has bioluminescence generated by spawning firefly squid pushed up by a unique current. In the Maldives they have biolumenescence on the beaches created by ostracod crustaceans. Ostracods produce a bright cloud of luminescence much larger than the ostracod itself. This cloud may scare or startle predators. Males use luminescence to attract females. Cypridina serrata eject luciferin and luciferase into sea water, creating the bioluminescence.

Bioluminescence may be other colors, too. And while it is almost non-existent in fresh water, it is not limited to beaches. One bioluminescent fish lights up surrounding water with a red glow in which only it can see, since it's predators and prey cannot see red.

The neon blue glow at the edge of the ocean does not occur every year in San Diego, California. It is seasonal even in the years it occurs. Travel anywhere to see biolumenescence should be contingent on checking likelihood and timing of the phenomenon prior to booking.


"Bioluminescence is a 'cold light.' Cold light means less than 20% of the light generates thermal radiation, or heat." - National Geographic, link below.
